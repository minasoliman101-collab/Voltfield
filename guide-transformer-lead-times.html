Why Transformer Lead Times Stretch Past 3 Years — And How to Plan Around It

If a project's one-line diagram has a large power transformer on it, the transformer — not the switchgear, not the generator, not permitting — is almost always the critical path now. Here's why, and what to do about it.

The lead times, in plain terms

Across the industry, large power transformers are quoting toward the 160-week mark, GSU transformers used at generation and substation sites are running around 144 weeks, and even the smaller pad-mount units common on distribution and data center jobs are quoting around 85 weeks. Some MV switchgear is effectively sold out into 2028. None of that is a typo or a worst-case outlier — it's the current baseline across North America and Europe.

What's actually driving it

Three things are compounding at once. First, a shortage of grain-oriented electrical steel (the core material every transformer is wound around) has constrained manufacturing capacity industry-wide. Second, the skilled-labor pool for winding and assembling large transformers hasn't grown to match demand — this isn't a job you can staff up for in a quarter. Third, and newest, is demand itself: AI data center buildouts and EV charging infrastructure have added a wave of orders on top of ordinary utility replacement and grid-upgrade demand.

That last point matters more than it might seem. Utilities and hyperscale data center developers are now bidding for the same limited pool of transformers and switchgear — which means a data center project isn't just competing with other data centers for capacity, it's competing with the utility that would otherwise be upgrading the substation feeding it.

The practical implication: if your project timeline assumes a transformer shows up in 6-12 months because that's what it used to take, that assumption is now the single biggest risk on the schedule — bigger than permitting, bigger than site work, often bigger than financing close.

What this means for your project timeline

The old sequencing — finalize design, then order long-lead equipment — doesn't work anymore. If a transformer order goes in after the one-line diagram is fully locked, you've already lost a year or more relative to a project that ordered against a preliminary spec and refined the configuration while the unit was in production. For anything with a 2027 or 2028 energization date, the transformer order effectively needs to happen now, not after the rest of the design is finalized.

Practical steps for procurement teams

  • Order against a preliminary spec, not a final one. Lock the electrical parameters that manufacturing actually needs (kVA, voltage class, impedance range) early, and negotiate minor spec refinements during the build rather than waiting to order until every detail is settled.
  • Split the BOM by lead time, not by system. Transformers, MV switchgear, and gas turbines belong in a separate, earlier procurement track from busway, panels, and MRO items that quote in weeks, not years — bundling them into one RFQ process means the fast items wait on the slow ones.
  • Ask about rebuilt and recertified units for interim capacity. For projects where a temporary or lower-capacity solution can bridge to the permanent unit's delivery, a rebuilt transformer with a materially shorter lead time can de-risk the schedule even at a pricing premium.
  • Re-check pad-mount vs. GSU tradeoffs early. The lead-time gap between transformer classes (roughly 85 weeks vs. 144+ weeks in the current market) is sometimes large enough to justify a design change if it's caught early enough to matter.
  • Get a real lead-time quote before the design freezes, not an assumption based on last cycle's numbers. Quoted lead time on a specific configuration is the only number worth planning against.

Frequently asked questions

How long are power transformer lead times right now?

Large power transformers are quoting toward 160 weeks industry-wide, GSU transformers around 144 weeks, and pad-mount transformers around 85 weeks — with some MV switchgear sold out into 2028.

Why are transformer lead times so long?

Three factors are compounding at once: a shortage of grain-oriented electrical steel, a skilled-labor bottleneck in winding and assembly, and a demand surge from AI data centers and EV charging that's competing with ordinary utility replacement demand.

Can I get a transformer faster with a rebuilt or recertified unit?

Often yes — a rebuilt or recertified unit can offer a materially shorter lead time for interim or lower-capacity needs, typically at a pricing premium over new.

When should I place a transformer order relative to finalizing my project design?

As early as possible, against a preliminary spec. Waiting until the full design is locked before ordering can add a year or more to the project timeline given current lead times.
