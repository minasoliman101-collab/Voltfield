FEOC Compliance for Solar & Storage Procurement: A Practical Guide

FEOC rules went from a footnote to a first-order sourcing decision in about two years. Here's what changed, what it means for a project chasing ITC eligibility, and what documentation to start gathering now.

Why FEOC suddenly matters

"FEOC" — Foreign Entity of Concern — restrictions determine whether a solar or storage project's components disqualify it from Investment Tax Credit (ITC) eligibility and domestic-content bonus credits. For years this sat in the background of procurement decisions. It doesn't anymore: developers now have to document component sourcing well enough to prove a project qualifies, not just assume it does.

What changed with Treasury's guidance

Treasury's first interim "material assistance" safe-harbor guidance gave developers a workable path for the first time — a defined way to document component sourcing and demonstrate ITC eligibility, rather than operating on incomplete rules and guesswork. That guidance eased months of procurement uncertainty, but it also formalized the expectation: buyers are now expected to trace and document sourcing at the component level, not just the module or system level.

The tariff and domestic-content overlay

FEOC compliance doesn't sit in isolation — it's layered on top of Section 301 tariffs and domestic-content incentives that are already reshaping where equipment gets made. Inverter manufacturers, including major names like Power Electronics and SMA Solar, have been expanding US production specifically to capture the domestic-content bonus credit, which only compounds the sourcing due diligence a buyer needs to do: a component can be tariff-exposed, FEOC-relevant, and domestic-content-bonus-eligible all at once, and those three questions don't always have the same answer.

That combination has also shown up directly in pricing — utility-scale module pricing has climbed as buyers weigh tariff exposure against domestic-content bonuses, with a looming Section 232 polysilicon tariff decision adding further uncertainty. In practice, the lowest sticker price and the lowest total compliance-adjusted cost are frequently not the same component.

The practical implication: "cheapest landed cost" and "ITC-eligible" are no longer the same sourcing question. A component that's cheaper before tax credits can easily be more expensive after them, once FEOC and domestic-content status are factored in.

What to start gathering now

  • Component-level origin documentation — not just final assembly location, but where cells, wafers, and key subassemblies were sourced, since "material assistance" thresholds are evaluated below the finished-product level.
  • Manufacturer FEOC-status attestations, refreshed per order rather than kept on file indefinitely — sourcing relationships and ownership structures change, and safe-harbor documentation needs to reflect the specific units being purchased.
  • A tariff-exposure check alongside the FEOC check, since Section 301 tariff classification and FEOC status are evaluated separately and a component can pass one test and fail the other.
  • Domestic-content bonus eligibility documentation where applicable, tracked separately from FEOC compliance since the two credit mechanisms have different qualification rules.
  • A sourcing decision record for every major component, made at time of purchase — reconstructing this after the fact for an ITC filing is far harder than documenting it during procurement.

What is FEOC and why does it matter for solar and storage projects?

What changed with Treasury's FEOC guidance?

Is FEOC compliance the same as domestic-content bonus eligibility?

No — they are separate qualification mechanisms evaluated independently. A component can pass one test and fail the other.
